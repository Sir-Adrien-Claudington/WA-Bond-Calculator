Fair wear and tear is NOT claimable

Under the Residential Tenancies Act 1987 (WA), a landlord cannot claim for fair wear and tear — the natural deterioration that happens through ordinary, reasonable use over time.

Not claimable (fair wear and tear):
  • Carpet worn thin in high-traffic areas
  • Minor scuffs and marks on walls
  • Paint faded by sunlight
  • Loose hinges or taps from normal use
Claimable (damage / neglect):
  • Burns, large stains, or tears in carpet
  • Holes punched in walls or doors
  • Broken fixtures from misuse
  • Deliberate or negligent damage

Understanding WA Rental Bonds

A rental bond is a security deposit paid by a tenant before moving into a rental property in Western Australia. Under the Residential Tenancies Act 1987 (WA), as amended by the Residential Tenancies Amendment Act 2024, the bond is held by the WA Bond Administrator — not the landlord — and can only be released by a formal application on Form 6.

Maximum bond amount

For standard residential tenancies, the maximum bond is four weeks’ rent, set by s 29 of the Residential Tenancies Act 1987 (WA). A landlord cannot require more than this regardless of the weekly rent amount.

What landlords can claim

Landlords may only claim from the bond for:

  • Unpaid rent
  • Cleaning costs beyond the property’s original condition
  • Damage caused by the tenant, beyond fair wear and tear
  • Replacement of lost keys, remotes, or access devices
  • Fumigation or pet-related damage (if a pet bond was charged, capped at $350)

Fair wear and tear

Fair wear and tear is the natural deterioration of a property through ordinary, reasonable use. It is not claimable. Examples include carpet worn thin in walkways, minor wall scuffs, and paint faded by sunlight. Burns, large stains, holes in walls, and deliberate damage go beyond fair wear and tear and are claimable.

The bond disposal process

At the end of a tenancy, either party lodges Form 6 (Application for Disposal of Security Bond) with the WA Bond Administrator. Both parties can agree to how the bond is split, or the matter can proceed to the State Administrative Tribunal of Western Australia (SAT) for a binding decision. Landlords must have lodged the bond with the Bond Administrator within 14 days of receipt.

Pet bonds

Where pets are approved, landlords may charge a pet bond of up to $350 total regardless of the number of pets. The pet bond can only be used for fumigation or pet-related damage — not general cleaning.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the maximum rental bond in WA?

The maximum bond is four weeks’ rent under the Residential Tenancies Act 1987 (WA) s 29. For example, at $500 per week the maximum bond is $2,000.

What can a landlord deduct from a bond in WA?

Unpaid rent, cleaning beyond the original condition, damage beyond fair wear and tear, replacement of lost keys or access devices, and pet bond costs (fumigation or pet-related damage, capped at $350). Fair wear and tear is not claimable.

What is fair wear and tear in WA?

The natural deterioration of a property through normal use. Not claimable. Examples: carpet worn in high-traffic areas, minor scuffs, faded paint. Burns, stains, holes, and deliberate damage are claimable.

What is the pet bond cap in WA?

$350 total, regardless of the number of pets. It can only be applied to fumigation or pet-related damage — not general cleaning.

What is Form 6 for rental bonds in WA?

Form 6 is the Application for Disposal of Security Bond — the official form used to claim or release a bond in WA. It is lodged with the WA Bond Administrator by the landlord, the tenant, or both parties jointly.

How do I dispute a bond claim in WA?

Apply to the State Administrative Tribunal of Western Australia (SAT). SAT makes binding decisions and hearings do not require legal representation.

How long does a landlord have to lodge the bond in WA?

14 days from the date of receipt, under the Residential Tenancies Act 1987 (WA).

Can a landlord keep the whole bond in WA?

Only if the total evidenced claims equal or exceed the bond amount. Any remaining bond must be returned to the tenant. If the claim exceeds the bond, the landlord must pursue the excess through SAT.
