Fair Wear and Tear in WA

What landlords can and cannot claim — with clear examples

Under the Residential Tenancies Act 1987 (WA), landlords cannot claim against a tenant’s bond for fair wear and tear. This is one of the most commonly disputed areas of WA tenancy law — and understanding it can mean the difference between getting your bond back in full or losing hundreds of dollars.

What is fair wear and tear?

Fair wear and tear is the natural, gradual deterioration of a property through ordinary and reasonable use over time. It is not damage — it is the expected ageing of surfaces and fixtures that happens when people simply live in a home. A landlord cannot require a tenant to return the property in a condition better than its original state at the start of the tenancy.

Examples: fair wear and tear (NOT claimable)

  • Carpet worn thin in high-traffic areas such as hallways and living rooms
  • Minor scuffs, light marks, or small scratches on walls from everyday living
  • Paint faded, yellowed, or slightly discoloured by sunlight or age
  • Loose door hinges, stiff taps, or sticky locks from normal use
  • Worn finish on benchtops from regular kitchen use
  • Faded curtains or blinds from sun exposure
  • Small nail holes from hanging standard picture frames

Examples: damage beyond fair wear and tear (claimable)

  • Burns, large stains, tears, or permanent discolouration on carpet
  • Holes punched or kicked in walls or doors
  • Deep gouges, graffiti, or extensive marks on walls requiring full repainting
  • Broken tiles, cracked glass, or smashed fixtures
  • Urine or pet stains soaked into flooring
  • Deliberate damage of any kind
  • Damage caused by neglect, such as mould from inadequate ventilation or unreported leaks

How age and quality affect the assessment

The longer a tenancy lasts and the older a property’s fittings are, the more deterioration is expected as fair wear and tear. A landlord replacing 15-year-old carpet at the end of a three-year tenancy cannot charge the tenant the full cost of new carpet — depreciation must be taken into account. Consumer Protection WA provides depreciation guidelines for common items such as carpet, paint, and blinds.

What to do if a landlord claims for fair wear and tear

If your landlord is claiming bond deductions for items you believe are fair wear and tear, you have the right to dispute the claim. Do not sign a Form 6 (Application for Disposal of Security Bond) you disagree with. Either party can apply to the State Administrative Tribunal of Western Australia (SAT) for a binding decision. SAT regularly rules against landlords who attempt to claim for fair wear and tear.
